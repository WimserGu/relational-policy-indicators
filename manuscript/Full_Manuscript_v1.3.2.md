# Does Shared-ASN Participation Align with AI Collaboration? Cross-Layer Evidence from African Country Networks

## Abstract

Shared-ASN participation counts autonomous systems recorded for one country that also appear in another observed African country. It is a relational PeeringDB measure, not bandwidth or a direct connection. This paper develops a framework for evaluating cross-layer correspondence among relational policy indicators and the observation rules that define their estimands. We apply it to 2021–2025 OpenAlex AI publications and coauthorship, a frozen 15 July 2026 PeeringDB snapshot, Regional Economic Community (REC) co-membership, and geographic and scale controls. Of 55 African Union states, 39 met the technical observation rule; the primary dyadic analysis covers 38 countries and 703 pairs. Across observed countries, the shared-ASN and knowledge layers were related but not structurally redundant. Country positions had a positive but modest rank correlation (Spearman rho = 0.334; median absolute percentile gap = 15.789), with substantial descriptive resampling uncertainty. The conditional shared-ASN co-presence estimate was consistently small and positive across 11 registered specifications, but uncertain (primary standardized estimate = 0.042; permutation p = 0.257). Knowledge ties were more concentrated within shared REC boundaries than under the fixed-degree reference (54.1% versus 43.2%), whereas the adjusted REC association was small and uncertain. PeeringDB observability was selective and narrowed the estimand. The results show partial cross-layer correspondence alongside substantial layer-specific structure. Proxy use therefore requires empirical validation for its intended application, while an indicator's source, snapshot, eligible node set, observation states, and denominator form part of its meaning.

**Policy significance statement**

Technical and publication indicators are often treated as interchangeable signs of AI participation. This study shows they should not be assumed substitutable without empirical validation for the intended use. Shared-ASN participation—how many autonomous systems recorded for a country also appear in another observed African country—was related to AI collaboration but captured only part of the same relational pattern. Public-data coverage determined which countries and pairs could be compared. Policy dashboards should preserve separate layers until a proxy relationship is validated and attach an observation contract specifying source, snapshot, eligible countries, recorded zeroes, non-observation, and denominators. They should distinguish regional clustering from evidence about REC institutions. These practices retain policy-relevant information and prevent documentation gaps from becoming performance rankings.

**Keywords:** policy-data governance; public-data observability; multilayer networks; Regional Economic Communities; network comparison


## 1. Introduction

Artificial intelligence policy is frequently organized around the country. Comparative assessments summarize national foundations, governance arrangements and research environments, giving governments and international organizations a practical view of where countries stand on selected attributes (Diallo et al. 2025; Salhi 2026). Yet AI research also develops through relationships that cross national borders. Scientific collaboration joins researchers and institutions, while recorded shared-ASN participation represents a different technical relation. Country-level attributes alone cannot show whether countries occupy comparable positions across these measured layers.

This distinction is particularly important in Africa. The continent's scientific production has been shaped by regional ties, international partnerships and historically uneven collaboration structures (Boshoff 2009; Sooryamoorthy 2020; Toivanen and Ponomariov 2011). A country with substantial observed AI publication activity may nevertheless hold a different position in an intra-African collaboration network. Conversely, a country with lower publication activity may participate in relationships that give it a more prominent network position. National AI research activity and relational position therefore answer different policy questions: one describes observed scholarly activity located in a country, while the other describes how countries are situated among one another.

A multilayer perspective makes this difference explicit. Networks can share the same nodes while representing relations with distinct meanings (Kivelä et al. 2014). In this study, national AI research activity records publication activity; shared-ASN participation position represents a country's percentile rank on the count of its ASNs also observed in at least one other C39 country; and cross-border AI collaboration-network position represents realized coauthorship relations. These measures should not be collapsed into a composite index. Their policy value lies partly in determining whether they convey similar or different information about countries' positions.

Prior research provides reasons to expect some correspondence between technical conditions and scientific relationships. Studies of digital communication, Internet access and information-technology adoption have linked changing communication environments to research participation, distributed knowledge work and collaboration patterns (Agrawal and Goldfarb 2008; Ding et al. 2010; Forman and van Zeebroeck 2019; Xu and Reed 2021). Li and Gao (2026) additionally examine scientific collaboration following activation of a major submarine cable. This literature establishes an important connection between digital conditions and knowledge production, but it does not establish that any particular technical indicator is an adequate substitute for a knowledge-network indicator. A discrete connectivity change, national Internet access and PeeringDB-recorded shared-ASN participation are different analytical objects. Nor does an association between technical and scientific conditions establish that one measure is a valid proxy for the other.

African collaboration research raises a second distinction. Scientific ties display regional, linguistic, geographic and extra-regional patterning, and REC boundaries overlap with several of these forms of proximity (Cassi et al. 2018; Dosso et al. 2023). Finding that many ties lie within shared REC boundaries does not, by itself, identify an independent institutional association. Empirical regional concentration describes where ties are located relative to a network reference, whereas an adjusted shared-REC association asks whether formal co-membership retains a conditional relationship after specified dyadic terms are considered. Treating these quantities as equivalent can turn network geography into an unsupported account of institutional performance.

A third issue concerns what public data make observable. PeeringDB and other public IXP datasets provide valuable views of the interconnection ecosystem, but their coverage and recorded fields differ (Klöti et al. 2016; Lodhi et al. 2014). Bibliographic databases likewise vary in geographical, linguistic and reference coverage (Céspedes et al. 2025; Culbert et al. 2025; Maddi et al. 2025). These boundaries are consequential because an unobserved country removes its possible dyadic relations. For PeeringDB-derived measures, `recorded positive`, `recorded zero under the frozen PeeringDB construction`, and `not observed under the source and eligibility rule` must remain separate states. A recorded zero does not prove real-world absence.

Taken together, these literatures leave a common measurement and policy-data gap. Relational indicators drawn from different public-data systems may concern the same countries and broad policy domain while encoding different relations. It remains less well established how much structure is shared across national AI research activity, shared-ASN participation position and cross-border AI collaboration, how much remains layer-specific, and how observation rules define the countries and dyads to which such comparisons refer. The gap therefore concerns both cross-layer proxy validation and the governance of relational-data observability. A further distinction is required between empirical regional concentration and adjusted formal co-membership.

The paper addresses one primary research question: **How do national AI research activity, shared-ASN participation positions, and cross-border AI collaboration networks align across infrastructure-observed African countries?** This wording deliberately combines a contextual country attribute with two measured relational positions without making the activity measure part of the shared-ASN–knowledge alignment decision. It also limits technical inference to countries meeting the frozen infrastructure-observation rule.

Empirically, the study links 2021–2025 OpenAlex publication and cross-border coauthorship records with the frozen 15 July 2026 PeeringDB IXP/ASN snapshot and REC co-membership. It constructs three independent country positions, compares shared-ASN participation and knowledge positions within C39, and estimates registered dyadic associations using network-aware permutation procedures. Uniform fixed-degree references distinguish empirical within-REC concentration from adjusted shared-REC association. A coverage audit preserves source-bounded states and compares the PeeringDB-observed node set with A55. The separately registered temporal module remains an exploratory assessment of next-year tie presence after two tie-free pre-event years.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it provides a measurement framework for evaluating cross-layer correspondence among relational policy indicators. Across the frozen country-position diagnostics and registered dyadic models, shared-ASN participation and AI collaboration contain related but non-redundant structure; their use as proxies for one another therefore requires empirical validation for the intended application. National AI research activity remains a contextual attribute rather than a third Gate A component. Second, the paper establishes observability as part of relational-indicator governance. It operationalizes an **observation contract** comprising source, snapshot, node-eligibility rule, observation state and denominator, and shows empirically that this contract changes the population represented by the indicator. Third, it separates empirical within-REC concentration from adjusted shared-REC association, demonstrating why network organization cannot be read directly as institutional attribution. The exploratory temporal extension remains outside these three contributions and retains `INSUFFICIENT HOLDOUT SUPPORT` for T1-B.

The remainder of the paper develops the opportunity–realization–observability framework, describes the data and registered analytical design, reports country-level and dyadic results, and discusses their implications for layer-specific AI monitoring. The central premise is simple: national activity, C39-defined shared-ASN participation position and knowledge collaboration are all policy-relevant, but their meaning depends on keeping their constructs, network layers and observation boundaries distinct.


## 2. Theoretical framework: opportunity, realization and observability across network layers

### 2.1 National attributes and relational positions

Country-level AI assessments commonly organize policy evidence around national attributes such as governance, digital foundations, research activity and institutional preparedness. This perspective is useful for comparing endowments and policy environments, including within Africa (Diallo et al. 2025; Salhi 2026). It does not, however, show how national systems are positioned in cross-border technical and knowledge relationships. A country may display substantial domestic AI research activity while occupying a peripheral position in an intra-African collaboration network; another may have modest publication activity but participate in relationships that give it a different regional position. National attributes and relational positions therefore answer different policy questions.

This study treats national AI research activity as a contextual country attribute, not as a comprehensive national AI indicator. Its relational objects are shared-ASN participation position at country level, PeeringDB-recorded shared-ASN co-presence at dyad level, and cross-border AI collaboration-network position. That distinction changes the unit of attention from the country considered in isolation to the pattern of relationships among countries. It is consistent with research showing that African scientific production is structured through regional, international and historically uneven collaboration networks rather than national publication totals alone (Boshoff 2009; Sooryamoorthy 2020; Toivanen and Ponomariov 2011).

A multilayer perspective is useful because the same countries can occupy different positions in networks whose ties have different meanings (Kivelä et al. 2014). Sharing a node set does not establish equivalence between layers. National research activity records observed publication activity; PeeringDB-recorded shared-ASN co-presence records overlap in documented ASN participation; coauthorship records realized joint knowledge production. The analytical task is therefore to compare these layers without collapsing them into a single country score.

### 2.2 Technical conditions, documented network context and realized knowledge relations

Research on communication technologies provides good reason to expect some relationship between digital conditions and knowledge production. Lower communication costs have been associated with wider participation in research, changes in collaboration patterns and reduced barriers to geographically distributed knowledge exchange (Agrawal and Goldfarb 2008; Ding et al. 2010; Forman and van Zeebroeck 2019; Xu and Reed 2021). Li and Gao (2026), using the activation of the Asia–Africa–Europe-1 submarine cable, further show that a discrete infrastructure change can be followed by increased scientific collaboration under identifiable contextual conditions. The general connection between digital infrastructure and research collaboration is therefore already established in adjacent literature.

The present technical measures represent narrower analytical objects. At country level, shared-ASN participation count records how many of a country's ASNs are also recorded in at least one other C39 country; its percentile is shared-ASN participation position. At dyad level, inverse-ubiquity-weighted shared-ASN co-presence describes overlap in the PeeringDB-recorded IXP/ASN ecosystem. Neither quantity records direct bilateral peering, traffic exchange, bandwidth, latency or use by research institutions. General theory may treat technical conditions as opportunities for interaction, but the measured quantities are based on a snapshot recorded on 15 July 2026 and do not establish that the measured technical relations preceded the 2021–2025 knowledge relations.

AI coauthorship additionally depends on scientific capabilities and complementarities, compatible research interests, organizational routines, partner discovery, mobility, funding and trust. Proximity research similarly distinguishes geographic, cognitive, organizational, social and institutional forms of proximity and cautions against treating any one form as sufficient for knowledge interaction (Boschma 2005). These conditions are not all measured in the present design and are not proposed as tested mechanisms. They explain why a technical relationship can be theoretically relevant without closely reproducing the topology of a knowledge-collaboration network.

The resulting framework distinguishes the general concept of **technical opportunity** from **relational realization**, while keeping the empirical timing boundary explicit. The former is theoretical; the measured quantities are C39-defined shared-ASN participation position and PeeringDB-recorded dyadic shared-ASN co-presence from 15 July 2026. Realization concerns observed 2021–2025 cross-border coauthorship. Their empirical comparison is a cross-sectional construct comparison across different observation windows, not evidence of temporal ordering.

### 2.3 Cross-layer correspondence and the policy problem of indicator substitution

Cross-layer correspondence asks how much national-position and dyadic structure is shared between relational measures and how much remains layer-specific. It does not require the two layers to be identical, nor does it treat one as a cause of the other. Shared-ASN participation and AI coauthorship represent different relations, but both may be used in policy accounts of AI participation. Their correspondence is therefore a measurement question about information overlap: does one indicator reproduce enough of the other layer's ordering and relational pattern to function as a proxy in a defined use?

This question matters because policy dashboards often combine heterogeneous indicators or use an available technical measure as shorthand for a less observable social or knowledge relationship. The present design evaluates correspondence at more than one level: rank correspondence and percentile gaps compare country positions, while dyadic models examine conditional similarity in bilateral structure. Gate A controls the strength of the country-level wording; it is not a formal equivalence test. Suitability as a proxy remains application-specific, but substitutability cannot be assumed simply because two indicators share a node set or policy label.

National AI research activity plays a deliberately bounded role in this comparison. It situates countries whose shared-ASN participation and knowledge positions are compared, but it does not enter Gate A as a third joint-test component. Keeping it contextual prevents a return to a composite score and preserves the distinction between domestic activity, shared-ASN participation and realized collaboration.

The paper therefore contributes a measurement framework for evaluating cross-layer correspondence among relational policy indicators. Its purpose is not to infer that one layer causes another, but to determine how much observed structure is shared across layers and how much remains layer-specific. Partial correspondence has a positive policy implication: until a proxy relationship is validated for a defined use, separate reporting preserves information that would be lost by collapsing distinct network layers.

### 2.4 Empirical regional concentration and adjusted REC association

African scientific collaboration has long displayed strong regional, linguistic, historical and extra-regional patterning (Boshoff 2009; Cassi et al. 2018; Dosso et al. 2023; Toivanen and Ponomariov 2011). REC boundaries overlap with several of these forms of proximity. A network may therefore place many observed ties within shared REC boundaries even when formal co-membership has only a limited residual association after geography, language, borders and scale are considered.

The distinction is analytical as well as substantive. A degree-preserving null asks whether the observed network locates more ties within shared REC boundaries than expected for a network with the same node degrees. It describes the arrangement of ties while retaining the observed prominence of hubs and peripheral countries. The adjusted dyadic model asks whether shared REC co-membership is associated with collaboration strength after registered covariates are included. The first quantity concerns empirical network concentration; the second concerns conditional association.

These quantities need not move together. Within-REC concentration can reflect overlapping geography, language, organizational histories, funding patterns or other forms of proximity without identifying an independent institutional contribution. Conversely, a conditional association would not by itself show that an REC produced the observed network structure. Treating the two analyses separately therefore prevents regional clustering from being read automatically as evidence of REC policy performance.

The second theoretical contribution is this structure–association distinction. It shifts the question from whether RECs “work” to which aspect of regional organization is being described: where observed ties are located, or whether formal co-membership retains a conditional dyadic association. This distinction allows apparently different empirical patterns to coexist without contradiction.

### 2.5 Observability and the estimand of relational policy

Relational policy analysis depends on which nodes and ties a data system makes visible. PeeringDB is an operator-maintained account of the public peering ecosystem, and peer-reviewed evaluations show that public IXP datasets differ in coverage and recorded detail (Klöti et al. 2016; Lodhi et al. 2014). Bibliographic databases also display geographical, linguistic and reference-coverage differences (Céspedes et al. 2025; Culbert et al. 2025; Maddi et al. 2025). Open data therefore reduce access barriers without eliminating selection in what is documented.

In a network setting, non-observation has consequences beyond ordinary missing values. If a country is not observed under the PeeringDB eligibility rule, its dyadic relations cannot be interpreted as recorded zeroes. Coding them as zero changes the node set, apparent topology and population to which the result refers. The relevant source-bounded distinction is among recorded positive, recorded zero under the frozen construction, and not observed under the source and eligibility rule.

Observability is consequently a core part of the estimand rather than a third-order data limitation. The primary shared-ASN comparisons concern countries observed under the PeeringDB eligibility rule, while A55 describes the coverage boundary. Selective visibility may reproduce a policy hierarchy if documentation blanks are silently replaced with zeroes. Evidence of uneven African representation in AI-policy assessment reinforces the importance of making this source boundary explicit (Diallo et al. 2025).

This paper defines the required metadata as an **observation contract**: source, snapshot, node-eligibility rule, observation state and denominator. For relational indicators, this contract determines which nodes and dyads exist in the estimand and how recorded values can be interpreted. Operationalizing recorded positive, recorded zero and not-observed states turns coverage from a technical footnote into a constitutive part of policy measurement.

### 2.6 Integrated framework and bounded expectations

The framework links three propositions without collapsing them. First, technical conditions and realized knowledge collaboration may be related, but substitutability cannot be assumed and requires decision-specific evidence. Second, empirical regional concentration and adjusted shared-REC association describe different network features and can diverge. Third, infrastructure observability defines which relational comparisons are estimable and how their target population must be described.

Together, these propositions support the paper's single primary research question: **How do national AI research activity, shared-ASN participation positions, and cross-border AI collaboration networks align across infrastructure-observed African countries?** They also establish the interpretive boundaries for the empirical analysis. Cross-layer estimates are associations rather than infrastructure mechanisms; regional concentration is not institutional attribution; and non-observation is not a substantive zero.

The exploratory temporal extension remains outside this three-part theoretical core. Its role is to assess whether a one-year outcome window supplies enough observed next-year ties after two tie-free pre-event years for validation. It does not estimate strict first formation unless the event-year tie is also absent, does not introduce a separate pathway from shared-ASN co-presence to collaboration, and leaves collaboration deepening as a separately registered future question.


## 3. Data and methods

### 3.1 Study design and inference frames

The study uses a multilayer relational design to compare national AI research activity, shared-ASN participation position and cross-border AI collaboration-network position. The main analysis is cross-sectional: OpenAlex research and collaboration measures cover 2021–2025, whereas the PeeringDB measures come from an identity-level snapshot retrieved on 15 July 2026. The snapshot is not treated as a pre-period exposure. Country-position comparisons evaluate cross-sectional correspondence, and dyadic models estimate conditional associations rather than temporal or causal relationships. A separately registered longitudinal module is retained only as an exploratory extension.

Four country frames have distinct roles. A55 contains the 55 African Union member states in the frozen mother frame and preserves PeeringDB non-observation. C39 contains countries with an assigned PeeringDB IXP and at least one status-ok member ASN under the primary snapshot rule. The country-level shared-ASN–knowledge comparison and complete shared-ASN co-presence matrix are defined only within C39. The primary continuous model contains 38 of those countries because Cabo Verde is a knowledge-network isolate and its association-strength denominator is undefined; the resulting complete matrix contains 703 dyads. T31 is the separate longitudinal core of countries observed in at least four of six historical PeeringDB snapshots. T31 does not replace C39 in cross-sectional analysis.

### 3.2 OpenAlex data and the research and knowledge layers

OpenAlex data were retrieved from `https://api.openalex.org/works` between 2026-07-14T17:01:50Z and 2026-07-14T17:11:08Z. Cursor paging began with `cursor=*`; each response's `meta.next_cursor` supplied the next request until the archive reached the API-reported count, producing 196 pages of at most 200 records. The complete filter was `from_publication_date:2015-01-01,to_publication_date:2025-12-31,primary_topic.subfield.id:1702,authorships.institutions.country_code:DZ|AO|BJ|BW|BF|BI|CV|CM|CF|TD|KM|CG|CD|CI|DJ|EG|GQ|ER|SZ|ET|GA|GM|GH|GN|GW|KE|LS|LR|LY|MG|MW|ML|MR|MU|MA|MZ|NA|NE|NG|RW|EH|ST|SN|SC|SL|SO|ZA|SS|SD|TZ|TG|TN|UG|ZM|ZW`. The selected fields were `id,doi,display_name,publication_year,publication_date,type,authorships,primary_topic,updated_date`. The filter therefore included only works whose OpenAlex **primary topic** was assigned to subfield 1702 (Artificial Intelligence); no secondary-topic rule was applied.

The extraction returned 39,189 records and 39,189 unique OpenAlex work IDs; no duplicate ID was discarded and DOI-, title- or version-level deduplication was not performed. All returned work types were retained: no document-type exclusion was applied. This includes, among the 35,916 payload-validated core-period works, 27,693 articles, 4,392 book chapters, 2,321 preprints, 545 reviews, 328 datasets, 109 books, 107 peer reviews, 98 dissertations, 94 conference papers, 79 reports, 58 errata, 42 other records, 21 editorials, 15 paratext records, 10 letters, two retraction-type records and two reference entries. The API selection did not retrieve a separate `is_retracted` flag, so no retracted-work exclusion beyond the retained work-type field was applied. Seven filter hits with no resolved A55 country in the returned payload were retained in audit data but excluded from all aggregates. The principal period contains 24,267 payload-validated works in 2021–2025; 11,649 enter 2015–2019, and 3,266 records from 2020 remain audit-only (Table S5).

Country and dyadic quantities use only resolved affiliation countries, with fractional denominators calculated over all resolved countries worldwide. If a work contains \(m\) resolved countries, each represented country receives \(1/m\). When \(m\geq2\), each unordered resolved-country pair receives \(2/[m(m-1)]\). A work with one resolved African country plus unresolved affiliation text contributes to that country's activity using the resolved-country denominator, is classified as resolution-uncertain rather than domestic-only, and creates no African dyadic edge unless a second resolved African country is present. Unresolved authorships never create a country or dyadic edge, but resolved affiliations on the same work remain eligible. Thus unresolved affiliations can contribute to country activity and, when at least two A55 countries are resolved, to dyadic weights. A complete-matrix zero means that no included resolved-country contribution created that edge; it does not rule out a relationship hidden by unresolved affiliations. Of 39,189 works, 3,202 carried at least one unresolved affiliation authorship; 170 of these still contributed to a core intra-African dyadic weight through other resolved affiliations.

The country-level knowledge position is A55 fractional weighted degree, \(\sum_{j\in A55} w_{ij}^{K}\). The dyadic continuous outcome is OpenAlex association strength,

\[
AS_{ij}=\frac{2Ww_{ij}^{K}}{s_i s_j},
\]

where \(W\) is the sum of all fractional Africa–Africa edge weights over the complete A55 graph in the period, and \(s_i=\sum_{j\in A55}w_{ij}^{K}\) and \(s_j\) are strengths on that same A55 graph. Extra-African countries are not graph nodes and do not enter \(W\) or the strength sums, although they enter the work-level resolved-country count \(m\) and therefore affect fractional weights. A55 countries excluded from C39 or C38 still contribute to \(W\) and relevant node strengths. For the primary C38 model, \(W\), \(s_i\), and \(s_j\) were calculated on the complete A55 Africa–Africa fractional graph, and the resulting association-strength matrix was then restricted to the 38 eligible countries; values were not recomputed after restriction. The registered model uses \(\log(1+AS_{ij})\). When either node has zero A55 collaboration strength, association strength is undefined rather than zero (Table S6).

### 3.3 PeeringDB data and the technical layer

The technical layer was constructed from the PeeringDB API snapshot retrieved on 15 July 2026 (PeeringDB 2026). PeeringDB is treated as an operator-maintained record of publicly documented IXP and network participation, not as a comprehensive infrastructure census. Countries were assigned from the exchange-country field. Facility country did not create country–ASN incidence. Status-ok exchange-membership records were retained, repeated IPv4/IPv6 or IXP records were collapsed to one ASN–country incidence, and remote versus local participation was not inferred. Because the snapshot postdates the 2021–2025 knowledge window, the cross-layer estimand is a cross-sectional construct comparison between positions recorded over different observation windows; the snapshot is not a pre-period exposure.

A country entered C39 when the snapshot contained both an assigned IXP and at least one status-ok member ASN. Within C39, let (k_a) denote the number of countries in which ASN (a) is observed. The primary dyadic technical weight is

\[
w_{ij}^{T}=\sum_{a\in A_i\cap A_j}\frac{1}{k_a-1}.
\]

At dyad level, this inverse-ubiquity construction downweights an ASN's contribution to each country pair when that ASN is recorded in many covered countries. A positive value is `recorded positive`; a zero is a `recorded zero under the frozen PeeringDB construction`. A dyad involving a country outside C39 is `not observed under the source and eligibility rule` and has no primary technical value. A recorded zero does not establish the real-world absence of technical relationships. Shared-ASN co-presence does not measure direct bilateral peering, traffic exchange, bandwidth, latency, a physical circuit, or use by research institutions.

The registered country aggregation is \(d_i^{C39}=\sum_{j\ne i}w_{ij}^{T}\). Algebraically,

\[
\begin{aligned}
d_i^{C39}
&=\sum_{j\ne i}\sum_{a\in A_i\cap A_j}\frac{1}{k_a^{C39}-1}\\
&=\sum_{a\in A_i:k_a^{C39}\ge2}\frac{k_a^{C39}-1}{k_a^{C39}-1}\\
&=\left|\{a\in A_i:k_a^{C39}\ge2\}\right|.
\end{aligned}
\]

The country-level metric is therefore the **cross-country-shared ASN participation count**: the number of a country's ASNs also observed in at least one other C39 country. It is exported as an integer and called the **shared-ASN participation position** when expressed as a C39 percentile rank. It is not ordinary weighted degree, inverse-ubiquity-weighted centrality, partner diversity or a sample-independent national characteristic. Registered alternative dyadic edge definitions include raw shared-ASN count, Jaccard similarity, cosine similarity, exclusion of ASNs observed in at least 25% or 50% of covered countries, and exclusion of IXPs with ambiguous facility geography. A continental zero lower bound is reported separately as a sensitivity; assigning zero outside the covered frame for that bounded calculation does not change the underlying observation state.

### 3.4 REC membership and dyadic controls

Formal regional alignment was constructed from membership in the eight African Union-recognized Regional Economic Communities (African Union 2026). The primary coding uses end-2025 membership, verified against the organizations' official membership pages on 15 July 2026. For each dyad, `shared_rec_count` records the number of common RECs and `shared_rec_any` indicates at least one shared membership. A registered start-2021 sensitivity restores the earlier ECOWAS and EAC membership configurations. These variables record formal co-membership, not programme participation or institutional implementation.

Geographic controls come from CEPII GeoDist (Mayer and Zignago 2011). Capital-to-capital distance is primary; population-weighted distance is a registered alternative. Contiguity and common official language are binary indicators. The CEPII legacy code `ZAR` was mapped to the Democratic Republic of the Congo. GDP and population come from the World Bank World Development Indicators (World Bank 2026). The frozen selection rule chose the latest year from 2021–2025 with joint GDP and population availability for at least 90% of C39; 2025 met the rule with complete C39 coverage. Dyadic scale terms are geometric means. The primary specification uses the natural logs of the GDP geometric mean and the member-ASN-count geometric mean; population replaces GDP in one registered alternative.

### 3.5 Country-position indicators and Gate A

The three country positions were constructed independently. National AI research activity is fractional AI publication output; the C39 cross-country-shared ASN participation count yields shared-ASN participation position; and knowledge position is A55 fractional coauthorship weighted degree. Research-activity and knowledge percentiles use A55 for display, whereas the shared-ASN percentile uses C39. Countries outside C39 retain blank shared-ASN values and are `not observed under the source and eligibility rule`.

For an observed value (x_i) in reference set (S), the ascending average-rank percentile is

\[
P_i^S=100\frac{R_{avg}(x_i;S)-1}{|S|-1}.
\]

Ties receive their mean occupied rank, and recorded zeroes under the frozen PeeringDB construction remain in the technical denominator. No log transformation, winsorization or prior standardization is applied. For the formal shared-ASN–knowledge comparison, both positions are reranked within C39. The absolute position gap is the absolute difference between these two common-sample percentiles.

Gate A is a one-directional claim-strength rule based on the full-precision Spearman correlation between shared-ASN participation and knowledge positions and the median absolute percentile gap. Stronger `cross-layer misalignment` wording is authorized only when rho is no greater than 0.50 and the median absolute gap is at least 25 percentile points. Otherwise, the manuscript states that the joint misalignment criterion was not met; it does not assign an automatic residual alignment category. The thresholds are investigator-defined interpretive heuristics, frozen on 15 July 2026 before construction and inspection of the Gate A country-position result; they are not literature-derived or simulation-estimated equivalence margins. National AI research activity is contextual and does not enter this joint rule. Observed values and descriptive uncertainty are reported in Results.

### 3.6 Infrastructure observability diagnostics

Observation state was coded before modeling. `Recorded positive` denotes a defined positive PeeringDB-derived value, `recorded zero under the frozen PeeringDB construction` denotes a defined value equal to zero, and `not observed under the source and eligibility rule` denotes a value unavailable under the frozen source and eligibility rule. Query completion alone did not establish technical observability, and a recorded zero did not establish real-world absence. All primary PeeringDB matrices were built exclusively within C39; dyads involving the other 16 A55 countries were not included among C39 recorded zeroes.

Coverage diagnostics compared C39 with not-observed countries using standardized mean differences for registered transformed country characteristics. Fractional research activity was transformed with \(\log(1+x)\); population and GDP used natural logs. The standardized mean difference was the covered-minus-not-observed mean difference divided by the pooled standard deviation. A descriptive logistic model used standardized log fractional research activity, standardized log population and AU-region indicators among complete cases. Its area under the receiver-operating-characteristic curve summarized group separation. Neither the standardized differences nor the propensity model was used to weight observations, impute technical values or reconstruct missing relations.

### 3.7 Conditional cross-layer models and registered alternatives

The primary continuous model estimates associations among the 38 infrastructure-observed countries with defined knowledge association-strength denominators; its complete matrix contains 703 dyads. Cabo Verde is excluded because its A55 knowledge strength is zero and the association-strength denominator is undefined. Predictors are inverse-ubiquity-weighted shared-ASN co-presence, shared REC count, contiguous border, log capital distance, common official language, log GDP geometric mean and log member-ASN geometric mean. Continuous predictors—the shared-ASN co-presence weight, distance and two scale terms—are z-scored before fitting; shared REC count and binary indicators remain on their original scales. With \(x_k^*=(x_k-\bar x_k)/SD(x_k)\) for continuous predictors and \(x_k^*=x_k\) otherwise, the fitted model is \(y=\alpha+\sum_k\beta_k x_k^*+\varepsilon\), where \(y=\log(1+AS)\). The reported fully standardized estimate is \(\beta_k SD(x_k^*)/SD(y)\): it equals \(\beta_k/SD(y)\) for pre-fit z-scored predictors and \(\beta_k SD(x_k)/SD(y)\) for original-scale REC-count or binary predictors. Pseudocode: `X_cont <- zscore(X_cont); beta <- OLS(y, X); beta_std[k] <- beta[k] * SD(X_fitted[k]) / SD(y)`. Table 2 labels the fitted coefficient scale separately from the fully standardized estimate.

Inference used the double-semi-partialling multiple-regression quadratic-assignment procedure (DSP-MRQAP; Dekker et al. 2007). For each target predictor, the algorithm residualized that predictor on the other predictors and an intercept, mapped the residuals to a symmetric matrix, and jointly permuted country rows and matching columns. The target coefficient and its \(t\) statistic were recomputed for 10,000 permutations. The manuscript reports the two-sided Monte Carlo permutation probability with plus-one correction, \((b+1)/(B+1)\), where \(b\) is the number of permuted absolute statistics at least as large as the observed statistic and \(B=10{,}000\). The master analysis seed was 20260714; deterministic model-specific seeds are retained in the result files.

Eleven continuous specifications were frozen before manuscript writing: the primary end-2025 GDP model; population replacing GDP; start-2021 REC membership; Jaccard, cosine and raw shared-ASN technical weights; exclusion of ASNs at the 25% and 50% ubiquity thresholds; population-weighted distance; exclusion of ambiguous IXPs; and the separately labelled continental zero lower bound. A logistic Y-QAP of binary knowledge-tie presence supplied an additional outcome-definition check, using the same C38 matrix, 703 dyads, 222 positive ties, controls and 10,000 joint row-and-column outcome relabelings. Its shared-ASN co-presence log-odds coefficient was positive (0.256857) but uncertain (Monte Carlo permutation p with plus-one correction = 0.131187). Because it reused C38, it did not retain the Cabo Verde isolate; no existing registered raw-weight or binary model supplies an isolate-inclusive estimand, which remains a limitation rather than grounds for a post hoc core model.

### 3.8 Regional concentration and adjusted REC association

Regional organization was evaluated in two ways. First, matrix QAP correlations compared `shared_rec_any` with binary knowledge adjacency and recorded-positive shared-ASN co-presence adjacency on the complete C39 dyads (Hubert and Schultz 1976). Pearson correlation was primary; because both matrices are binary, average-rank Spearman correlation is algebraically identical. Two-sided Monte Carlo permutation probabilities with plus-one correction used 10,000 joint row-and-column relabelings.

Second, fixed-degree reference networks assessed whether a greater share of observed ties fell within shared REC boundaries than expected for a labelled simple graph with the same node degrees. Following the methodological audit, the infrastructure degree sequence was evaluated by exact equal weighting of all 63 labelled realizations. Completeness was verified by exhaustive breadth-first traversal of every valid 2-switch using a canonical adjacency bitset and duplicate removal; the switch-state graph is connected, and the general alternating-circuit argument for degree-sequence realizations implies that no additional switch components exist. The knowledge network used two independently seeded proposal-step double-edge-switch chains targeting the uniform fixed-degree ensemble, with invalid proposals retained as self-loops. The retained burn-in and spacing were accepted only after checking degree preservation, between-chain agreement, effective sample size and retained-draw autocorrelation. Observed diagnostic values are reported in Table S7 rather than used as substantive Results. The knowledge probability is a Monte Carlo upper-tail probability with plus-one correction, not an exact probability. For the descriptive weighted comparison, positive observed weights were randomly reassigned across each reference topology; binary degrees and the global positive-weight multiset are preserved, but node strengths, weighted-degree sequences and weight–endpoint association are not.

The structural quantities are the observed within-REC share, the null mean, their ratio and an upper-tail probability. Probability terminology follows the computation: exact enumeration for the 63-state infrastructure binary ensemble, and Monte Carlo probability for the sampled knowledge and descriptive weighted nulls. The adjusted shared-REC association is instead the `shared_rec_count` coefficient from the primary DSP-MRQAP. The procedures address different quantities and are not substitutes.

### 3.9 Influence and stability audits

Country influence was assessed under a plan locked before the audit. For the primary cross-layer model, each of the 38 eligible countries was deleted in turn; the complete dyadic matrix was rebuilt, continuous predictors were restandardized, and the model was refitted with 10,000 permutations. For regional QAP, each C39 country was deleted in turn and binary knowledge and shared-ASN co-presence correlations and within-REC shares were recomputed. A sign or 5% decision was country-stable when retained in at least 90% of deletion runs.

OLS leverage, studentized residuals, Cook's distance and approximate DFBETAS were calculated only as descriptive dyad rankings. They were not used for dyadic inference or post hoc exclusion. No country or dyad was removed from the reported primary model because of these diagnostics.

### 3.10 Exploratory temporal extension

T1 examined PeeringDB year-end transitions within the frozen T31 core. A dyad was comparable only when both countries had an active IXP and valid ASN membership in both adjacent snapshots. `Entry` indicated a shared-ASN count changing from zero in the prior snapshot to positive at year-end \(t\); `stable zero` indicated zero in both snapshots. The primary risk set required no AI coauthorship tie in \(t-2\) or \(t-1\), and the outcome was **next-year tie presence after two tie-free pre-event years**, defined as positive tie presence in \(t+1\). Because absence in event year \(t\) was not required, this is not a strict first-formation outcome.

The T1 linear-probability model included event-year fixed effects, log capital distance, contiguity, common official language, the dyadic mean and absolute gap of \(\log(1+x)\) fractional AI output in \(t-1\), and the corresponding mean and absolute gap of \(\log(1+x)\) unique ASN counts in the prior snapshot. Inference included HC3, a dyadic node-cluster score covariance with dyad-cluster subtraction, leave-one-country-out estimates and 10,000 country-label permutations with seed 20260715. Each permutation reconstructed the risk set, outcome and pre-event AI-size controls while holding PeeringDB transitions and geography fixed. In the primary risk set, 29 observations were positive in \(t+1\); six were already positive in \(t\), comprising five of 13 entry-group positives and one of 16 stable-zero positives. T1 remained `EXPLORATORY ONLY` under its prespecified decision rule.

T1-B applied the frozen design to the 2023–2024 PeeringDB exposure interval reserved for validation and the 2025 OpenAlex outcome, unused in T1 model development. It used the same 31-country core, required no knowledge tie in 2022 or 2023, and estimated a single-cohort model without year fixed effects. Country-label permutations used seed 2026071501. Before interpreting an estimate, the registered protocol required at least 20 entry dyads, 50 stable-zero dyads, 10 positive outcomes and a full-rank adjusted design. Failure of any gate required `INSUFFICIENT HOLDOUT SUPPORT`.

After the reserved-interval decision was fixed, the outcome-rarity audit introduced no new model. For event cohorts 2019–2024, the strict risk set additionally required no knowledge tie in event year (t). It summarized one-year formation and, where follow-up existed, cumulative formation by (t+2). Collaboration deepening was audited only to determine feasibility for a separately registered future question; it was not substituted for the frozen formation outcome.

### 3.11 Reproducibility and analytical boundaries

All source extracts, processed matrices, protocols, analysis scripts, permutation distributions, QA reports and SHA-256 manifests were retained by phase. OpenAlex retrieval used the complete frozen query specification and cursor-page archive described above. PeeringDB records, REC source registers, CEPII controls and World Bank responses were stored with retrieval or reference dates. The substantive fixed-degree null was prospectively specified, but the original implementation advanced the chain by accepted swaps. Following the 16 July 2026 methodological audit, it was corrected to a proposal-step chain with rejected proposals retained as self-loops, and the infrastructure sequence was evaluated by exact 63-state enumeration. Both original and corrected code and outputs are preserved; `REGISTERED_ANALYSIS_DEVIATIONS_v1.3.md` records dates, hashes and status. The workspace has no Git repository, so no commit hash exists; file hashes provide the immutable provenance identifiers.

The analyses were implemented in versioned Python and JavaScript scripts and independently checked against frozen input files. The final replication package will record the reconstruction environment and execution order. Public metadata were analyzed at country and country-pair level; no individual-level inference was conducted. The cross-sectional estimates are associational and do not identify causal effects, policy impacts or infrastructure-to-collaboration conversion.

The design supports cross-layer construct comparison, conditional dyadic association, regional-structure comparison and explicit observability analysis. These components evaluate shared and layer-specific structure without identifying a technical mechanism, REC policy performance, unobserved infrastructure relations or a temporal conversion process.


## 4. Results

### 4.1 Analytic frame and infrastructure observability

The continental mother frame comprised 55 African Union member states (Table 1). Although every PeeringDB country query completed, query completion did not establish technical-layer observability. Forty-two countries had at least one assigned IXP record, and 39 met the frozen C39 rule requiring both an assigned IXP and at least one status-ok member ASN. The remaining 16 countries were `not observed under the source and eligibility rule`. Within C39, 36 countries had a positive shared-ASN participation count and three had a recorded count of zero; its 741 country pairs comprised 513 recorded-positive and 228 recorded-zero dyads under the frozen PeeringDB construction. The primary continuous model retained 38 countries with defined knowledge association-strength denominators and 703 dyads after Cabo Verde's zero A55 knowledge strength made its denominator undefined.

Observability was strongly selective across measured country characteristics (Figure 3). Standardized mean differences between covered and not-observed countries ranged from 0.969 to 1.012 across fractional AI research activity, population and GDP, while the descriptive coverage-propensity AUC was 0.885. These diagnostics were not used for weighting or reconstruction of unobserved relations. The remaining shared-ASN results therefore concern countries observed under the PeeringDB rule, not the full continental frame.

### 4.2 National positions and cross-layer alignment

Across C39, shared-ASN participation position and AI collaboration-network position showed positive but modest rank correspondence. Their full-precision percentile ranks had a Spearman correlation of 0.333739, and the median absolute shared-ASN–knowledge percentile gap was 15.789474 points (Figure 1; Table S1). The correlation condition for stronger misalignment wording was satisfied but the median-gap condition was not, so the joint misalignment criterion was not met. A 10,000-draw paired country bootstrap produced descriptive percentile intervals of -0.050 to 0.637 for rho and 5.263 to 30.263 for the median gap; leave-one-country-out ranges were 0.280–0.401 and 14.474–15.789, respectively. Resampling therefore indicates substantial uncertainty around the magnitude of rank correspondence. National AI research activity provides context but is not part of Gate A.

The activity-position display was not materially sensitive to replacing fractional output with raw AI-work counts: both rank correlations exceeded 0.99, and no country shifted by at least 20 percentile points. Taken together with the dyadic evidence below, the country-level pattern is one of partial rather than redundant correspondence. The observed evidence does not justify treating shared-ASN participation and collaboration-network position as interchangeable by default; empirical validation is required for the intended proxy use.

### 4.3 Conditional cross-layer association and registered robustness

The registered primary DSP-MRQAP estimated a small positive conditional association between inverse-ubiquity-weighted shared-ASN co-presence and cross-border AI collaboration strength among the 38 infrastructure-observed countries with defined knowledge association-strength denominators (703 dyads; standardized estimate = 0.041668; Monte Carlo permutation p with plus-one correction = 0.256974; Table 2). Across the 11 registered continuous specifications, the standardized estimate remained positive, ranging from 0.005698 to 0.050100. The direction was consistent, while uncertainty remained substantial.

The country-deletion audit showed the same pattern: all 38 leave-one-country-out runs retained a positive estimate, ranging from 0.015938 to 0.054435, without a sign reversal. Selected registered alternatives—including population in place of GDP, Jaccard shared-ASN weights, exclusion of ambiguous IXPs and the separately labelled continental zero lower bound—remained similar in direction and magnitude (Table 2; Table S2). The lower-bound specification does not constitute an observed continental shared-ASN matrix.

Among the registered adjustments, common official language, contiguous border and GDP scale were positive, while capital distance was negative; the member-ASN scale estimate was small and uncertain (Table 2). Overall, the conditional evidence points to a consistently small positive association whose magnitude was stable across the registered checks but remained uncertain.

### 4.4 Empirical regional concentration and adjusted REC association

The knowledge-collaboration network was disproportionately concentrated within shared REC boundaries relative to the corrected uniform fixed-degree reference (Figure 2 and Table 3). Of 222 observed knowledge ties, 54.1% joined countries sharing at least one REC, compared with a switch-chain null mean of 43.2066%. The observed-to-null ratio was 1.251061, equivalent to 25.106% excess concentration (Monte Carlo upper-tail p with plus-one correction = 0.000100). The corresponding unadjusted QAP correlation was small and positive (r = 0.116; Monte Carlo permutation p with plus-one correction = 0.017698). All 39 country-deletion estimates remained positive, and 38 of 39 retained the full-sample 5% decision.

Recorded shared-ASN co-presence evidence differed between tie presence and tie weight. For the 513 recorded-positive binary dyads, the within-REC share was 49.5127%, compared with an exact uniform 63-state mean of 49.0052%. The observed-to-null ratio was 1.010355, or 1.035% above the null mean; two of 63 states were at least as extreme as observed (exact enumeration p = 0.031746). Although the unadjusted QAP correlation was positive (r = 0.130; Monte Carlo permutation p with plus-one correction = 0.035196), only 32 of 39 country-deletion runs retained the full-sample 5% decision, below the frozen 90% stability threshold. The binary pattern was substantively small and country-fragile. For shared-ASN co-presence weights, the observed within-REC share was 47.8145%, below the descriptive null mean of 48.9578% (ratio = 0.976648; descriptive Monte Carlo upper-tail p with plus-one correction = 0.552045). This weighted null preserves binary degrees and the positive-weight multiset, not node strengths.

The fixed-degree concentration analysis and the adjusted shared-REC term answer different questions. In the registered primary DSP-MRQAP, the shared-REC-count coefficient was −0.044624, with a standardized estimate of −0.045809 and a Monte Carlo permutation *p* with plus-one correction of 0.292671. This small negative estimate carried substantial uncertainty. It neither replaces nor negates the concentration of observed knowledge ties within shared REC boundaries: one describes network arrangement relative to a fixed-degree reference, while the other describes conditional dyadic association.

### 4.5 Exploratory temporal extension

The reserved temporal check could not validate a temporal association. T1 measured next-year tie presence after two tie-free pre-event years rather than strict first formation because six of its 29 next-year positives were already present in event year \(t\). Under the strict T1-B definition, no 2025 formations occurred, requiring the locked decision `INSUFFICIENT HOLDOUT SUPPORT`. The module does not strengthen the cross-sectional findings; risk-set attrition, cohort rates, and the strict-versus-compatible definitions are reported in Tables S3–S4.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Cross-layer information overlap and indicator substitution

The country and dyadic results describe a common pattern: shared-ASN participation and AI collaboration are related, but they retain substantial layer-specific structure. Within C39, country positions had a positive but modest rank correlation, with descriptive resampling showing considerable uncertainty around its magnitude. At dyad level, inverse-ubiquity-weighted shared-ASN co-presence remained positively associated with collaboration strength across all registered specifications, but the estimates were consistently small and uncertain. The technical and knowledge layers therefore show partial correspondence rather than structural redundancy.

This pattern is scientifically informative without a causal interpretation. Cross-layer validation asks whether two relational indicators encode sufficiently overlapping national rankings and bilateral relations to support a proxy use. The results show that a shared policy label—AI participation—and a common set of country nodes do not make the measured relations informationally interchangeable. Suitability for a particular decision would still require an application-specific criterion, but the default assumption of substitutability is not supported by the observed evidence.

National AI research activity supplies a third, contextual view without changing that cross-layer conclusion. Keeping publication activity separate from both relational layers prevents the analysis from becoming a composite ranking and preserves the distinction between domestic scholarly activity and relational position. Separate reporting is therefore an information-preserving design principle: until a proxy relationship is validated, collapsing these layers would conceal differences that the measurements reveal.

### 5.2 Regional organization without institutional attribution

The regional results demonstrate why network arrangement and institutional association must be interpreted separately. AI knowledge ties are concentrated within shared REC boundaries relative to a degree-preserving reference network. Yet the adjusted shared-REC association is small, negative and uncertain once the registered dyadic terms are included. These findings are not contradictory. The concentration analysis asks where ties are located while preserving each country's observed degree; the adjusted model asks whether shared formal membership retains a conditional association with tie strength. Each quantity describes a different feature of the same network.

This distinction is especially important in African research systems, where geography, language, organizational histories and extra-regional relationships overlap with formal regional boundaries (Boshoff 2009; Cassi et al. 2018; Dosso et al. 2023; Toivanen and Ponomariov 2011). Within-REC concentration may reflect several overlapping forms of proximity. The present analysis does not isolate which of them accounts for the arrangement. Equally, uncertainty in the adjusted co-membership estimate does not show that RECs have no role. It means only that the registered model does not provide a precise independent shared-REC association after its included terms are considered.

The PeeringDB-recorded shared-ASN layer reinforces the need for layer-specific interpretation. Its binary dyads show only a very small excess of within-REC concentration and fail the frozen country-deletion stability threshold, while the weighted pattern lies below its null mean. Formal regional boundaries therefore organize observed knowledge ties and shared-ASN co-presence differently. Analysts should not transfer a regional pattern across layers or treat regional clustering as REC policy performance.

These results refine how regional collaboration should be monitored. A descriptive question about whether ties fall within formal regional boundaries is useful for mapping the geography of a network. A conditional question about co-membership is useful for assessing whether formal alignment carries a residual association under a specified model. Reporting both prevents a concentrated regional pattern from being over-attributed to institutions and prevents an uncertain adjusted term from erasing the observed regional organization of the network.

### 5.3 The observation contract as policy-data governance

The coverage audit is a substantive result. The shared-ASN analysis concerns the 39 countries meeting the frozen PeeringDB observation rule, not a census of continental technical relations, and observed and not-observed countries differ systematically across measured characteristics. Observation rules therefore do more than produce missing values: they determine which nodes and dyads exist in the estimand.

Public datasets remain valuable precisely because they enable otherwise difficult cross-country comparisons. Openness, however, does not eliminate variation in who and what is recorded. Public IXP sources differ in coverage and fields (Klöti et al. 2016; Lodhi et al. 2014), while bibliographic databases vary in geographical, linguistic and reference coverage (Céspedes et al. 2025; Culbert et al. 2025; Maddi et al. 2025). These differences become part of the comparison when network nodes and dyads depend on source eligibility.

The distinction among `recorded positive`, `recorded zero under the frozen PeeringDB construction`, and `not observed under the source and eligibility rule` is therefore constitutive rather than cosmetic. Replacing non-observation with zero would change the apparent network, node set and population addressed by the estimates. A recorded zero also remains a source-bounded value, not proof of real-world absence.

The observation contract provides a practical governance rule for relational indicators: report the source, snapshot, node-eligibility rule, observation state and denominator with the value. A dashboard may appear continent-wide while a network measure represents only a selected node set. Carrying the observation contract into every comparison makes that estimand visible and prevents absence of documentation from becoming an implicit performance score.

### 5.4 Implications for AI monitoring and regional policy

The findings support an information-preserving approach to AI monitoring. National AI research activity, C39-defined shared-ASN participation position and cross-border AI collaboration-network position should be displayed separately until a proxy relationship has been validated for its intended use. This is not a fallback caused by an inconclusive test. It is a positive design rule derived from the observed partial correspondence: separate layers retain policy-relevant structure that would be lost if they were collapsed prematurely.

Three implementation rules follow. First, every PeeringDB-derived shared-ASN value should carry an observation contract so that recorded zero remains distinguishable from non-observation. Second, comparisons should identify the node set and denominator for each layer. Third, regional summaries should report empirical concentration separately from adjusted shared-REC association. Together, these rules connect construct validity, data coverage and the population represented by a policy indicator.

The evidence informs indicator design rather than a particular investment or institutional intervention. Inverse-ubiquity-weighted shared-ASN co-presence is a PeeringDB-recorded overlap measure, not service quality or scholarly use, and REC analyses describe network arrangement and conditional association rather than institutional performance. Its policy use is therefore to validate proxy relationships, preserve layer-specific information and make observation boundaries explicit.

For regional organizations, this framework also separates two possible monitoring tasks. One is to track whether observed collaboration is regionally concentrated. The other is to evaluate particular programmes, funding instruments or coordination arrangements using designs and data constructed for those interventions. The present paper informs the first task and clarifies why it cannot answer the second. That separation makes future evaluation questions more precise.

### 5.5 Limitations and research agenda

Several limitations bound these interpretations. First, PeeringDB is operator-maintained and provides a partial view of the public peering ecosystem. Shared-ASN participation count and dyadic shared-ASN co-presence do not measure direct bilateral peering, traffic, bandwidth, latency or use by research institutions. Recorded zeroes are constructed zeroes within the C39 PeeringDB matrix, not proof of real-world absence.

Second, OpenAlex supplies broad bibliographic coverage, but databases vary in geographical, linguistic and citation representation. All returned work types were retained, no separate retraction flag was retrieved, and unresolved affiliations can hide countries or edges even though resolved affiliations on the same work remain usable. Coauthorship captures formal joint publication, not informal exchange or every form of knowledge relation. The C38 continuous and binary models both exclude the Cabo Verde isolate because the existing sensitivity reused the continuous estimation matrix; no registered isolate-inclusive outcome model is available.

Third, REC co-membership is a coarse institutional indicator. Memberships overlap, and a shared formal boundary does not measure programme participation, implementation quality, funding exposure or the history of a specific partnership. The adjusted shared-REC term should accordingly remain a conditional association rather than an institutional evaluation.

Fourth, the principal cross-layer analysis compares 2021–2025 activity with a 15 July 2026 PeeringDB snapshot and is associational. It evaluates construct correspondence across different observation windows rather than temporal sequence. Institution-level linkages, researcher mobility, funding, cognitive proximity and organizational partnerships would require separately specified data and registered designs.

Finally, the temporal extension remains exploratory. T1 measures next-year tie presence after two tie-free pre-event years and is not identical to strict first formation; T1-B remains `INSUFFICIENT HOLDOUT SUPPORT`. Historical strict cohorts motivate longer follow-up but do not establish a fixed lag or future collaboration. Collaboration deepening would require a separately registered future design.

### 5.6 Synthesis

The paper provides a cross-layer validation and observation-governance framework for relational policy indicators. Applied to African AI research systems, it finds related but non-redundant structure across shared-ASN participation and knowledge collaboration: modest positive country-rank correspondence, consistently small positive dyadic estimates with substantial uncertainty, and different regional organization across layers. The appropriate conclusion is not universal non-equivalence. It is that a proxy relationship cannot be assumed from a shared node set or policy label and must be validated for its intended use.

The second core contribution is the observation contract. Source, snapshot, node eligibility, observation state and denominator define which countries and dyads enter the estimand and how a recorded value can be interpreted. The REC analysis adds a third distinction by separating empirical regional concentration from adjusted institutional association. Together, these contributions show why relational policy measurement must preserve differences among constructs, layers and observation boundaries. Separate reporting retains information until substitution has been justified; transparent observation contracts make clear whose relations the indicator actually represents.
